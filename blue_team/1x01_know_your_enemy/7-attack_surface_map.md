# The Attack Surface

## Introduction

### Goal
Systematically map MedDefense's attack surface across three dimensions: external, internal and human.

### Context
An attack surface is every point where an attacker could attempt to interact with your systems, your data or your people. It is not the same as a vulnerability. A locked door is part of the attack surface. A locked door with a broken lock is a vulnerability on that surface. Understanding the surface tells you where to look. Finding the vulnerabilities tells you what to fix.

## Answer

### Section 1: External Surface

MedDefense’s external surface is concentrated in a small number of Internet-facing services, but each one leads to a high-value internal asset or identity path. The main concern is not just exposure; it is that the same weak identity, patch, and recovery posture identified in 1x00 still sits behind those entry points.

| Entry point | What it is | Asset behind it | Existing protection from 1x00 | Documented gap from 1x00 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Patient portal | Public patient access portal hosted on the web application stack | [A-032 Patient-Portal-Service](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md), supported by [A-011 web-srv-01](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md) and the EHR data path | Perimeter firewalling (C-001), firewall logging (C-002), system logging (C-012), password policy (C-005), account lockout (C-006); limited MFA was noted in the posture assessment | GAP-016 for exposed internet-facing systems, GAP-017 for insufficient MFA, and GAP-023 for legacy TLS on the portal |
| Public website | Public-facing hospital website and patient-facing web services | [A-011 web-srv-01](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md) | Perimeter firewalling (C-001), firewall logging (C-002), system logging (C-012), security awareness training (C-011) for users who may receive spoofed web links | GAP-016 because the public web tier lacks mature vulnerability/patch governance; GAP-004 because logs are not centrally monitored; GAP-023 because the same host also carries patient-portal exposure |
| VPN endpoints | Remote access paths used by staff and site connectivity, including the FortiGate path into Central and the site-to-site links from Westside and HQ | [A-013 FortiGate-100F](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md), plus the Westside and HQ connectivity paths into the internal network | Perimeter firewalling (C-001), firewall logging (C-002), password policy (C-005), account lockout (C-006), and limited MFA noted in the posture assessment | GAP-016 for internet-facing patch governance, GAP-017 because MFA is not deployed at scale, GAP-006 because a VPN foothold can reach a flat internal network, and GAP-004 because follow-on activity is unlikely to be detected quickly |
| Email / O365 | Microsoft 365 email and collaboration services accessible from the Internet | [A-033 Microsoft 365 Tenant](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md) and organization-wide mailboxes | Password policy (C-005), account lockout (C-006), system/security logging (C-012), security awareness training (C-011), and limited MFA for only one account in the posture assessment | GAP-013 because retained accounts remain usable, GAP-014 because data export monitoring is missing, and GAP-017 because remote access and cloud access lack MFA at scale |
| DNS / name resolution | Public DNS records and name resolution that direct users to the portal and website | Internet-facing records that resolve to web-srv-01 and related services | Perimeter firewalling (C-001), firewall logging (C-002), and basic administrative oversight through IT operations | GAP-016 because externally reachable services do not have explicit vulnerability governance, and GAP-004 because there is no centralized monitoring to spot DNS-based abuse, lookalike infrastructure, or suspicious query patterns |
| Other externally reachable service | Public perimeter management and remote service exposure identified during the scan, especially any service terminating on the firewall or DMZ host | [A-013 FortiGate-100F](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md) and the DMZ host chain | Perimeter firewalling (C-001), firewall logging (C-002), and system logging (C-012) | GAP-016 and GAP-017, because any exposed management or remote-access service becomes a direct entry point when patching and MFA are incomplete |

### Section 2: Internal Surface

The internal surface is materially worse than the external one because MedDefense runs a flat 10.10.0.0/16 network with no VLAN segmentation. Once an attacker gets inside, they can see too much, reach too much, and often move laterally without meaningful barriers. That is why the same network scan results that might look ordinary on a segmented enterprise network become dangerous here.

| Asset | Exposure from the scan | Why this matters in a flat network |
|---|---|---|
| [A-004 billing-srv-01](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md) | MySQL service reachable across the network | Billing data becomes broadly reachable from any compromised workstation or VPN session. In a flat network, a single foothold can enumerate and attack the claims system without crossing a segmentation boundary. |
| [A-002 ehr-db-01](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md) | PostgreSQL service reachable across the network | The EHR database is the system of record for Restricted clinical data. Network-wide reachability means a compromised internal host can attempt database abuse, credential attacks, or data theft. |
| Local NAS / backup storage | NAS management and file services are reachable from internal hosts | Because backups and production systems share the same general network space, an attacker who reaches the NAS can target both operational data and recovery copies. This directly amplifies GAP-005. |
| [A-013 FortiGate-100F](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md) admin surface | Administrative interface and configuration plane | If the firewall management plane is reachable internally, any compromised internal account can change policy, create tunnels, or weaken controls. In a flat network, the firewall becomes another reachable host rather than a hardened boundary. |
| Medical IoT web interfaces | Pump, monitor, and other device management web interfaces | Default or weakly controlled management planes on medical devices are especially dangerous in a flat network because they sit beside clinical workstations and can be abused for persistence or patient-safety harm. |
| [A-021 MRI-CTRL-WS (WS-RAD-01)](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md) | Windows XP legacy workstation and its companion imaging path | The workstation is unsupported and operationally critical. With no segmentation, compromise can spread from the radiology lane into other internal systems, turning a legacy device into an enterprise foothold. |
| [A-008 print-srv-01](../1x00_first_watch/7-asset_registry.md) | Windows Server 2012 R2 print services | End-of-life infrastructure is a quiet pivot point: it is often trusted, lightly monitored, and reachable by many users. In a flat network, it becomes an easy stepping stone for lateral movement. |
| PACS / radiology services | PACS service and associated imaging workflow interfaces | Shared credentials and broad internal reach turn a radiology system into both a target and a launch point. If attackers can reach it, they can steal imaging data or pivot through the workflow toward clinical assets. |
| Default-credential IoT and device admin pages | Management pages on pumps, monitors, or other connected devices | Default credentials are a direct bypass of network trust. On a flat network, they let an attacker move from basic access to device control with little resistance. |

The key internal finding is that segmentation is absent. That means internal exposure is not just about the individual service; it is about the lack of containment between services. GAP-006 is the core issue behind every row above.

### Section 3: Human Surface

People are part of the attack surface because MedDefense’s workflows depend on busy staff, broad legitimate access, and manual exceptions. The most likely human attacks are not exotic; they are phishing, impersonation, account abuse, and pressure on staff who are expected to help quickly.

| Role | What they can access | Why they are targetable | Training or control gap that increases risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Clinical staff | EHR records, medication data, lab results, imaging, schedules, and bedside systems | They work under time pressure, interact with urgent patient issues, and routinely receive requests that appear operationally legitimate. That makes them vulnerable to phishing, tailgating, and session hijacking. | GAP-001 because unattended EHR sessions can be viewed or altered at the point of care; GAP-014 because bulk or unusual access is not monitored well enough to catch misuse; GAP-004 because suspicious behavior is unlikely to be centralized quickly |
| Reception / front desk | Building entry, visitor handling, phone routing, badge-facing interactions, and first-contact information | Reception is the first stop for outsiders and therefore a natural target for impersonation, pretexting, and tailgating. Attackers exploit helpfulness and routine access-control bypasses. | C-009 visitor access control exists, but the broader weakness is GAP-004: if an adversary gets past reception, there is no strong centralized detection to catch the mistake quickly |
| IT staff | Privileged access to servers, network devices, endpoints, identity systems, and remote management tools | The team is small, busy, and fatigue-prone. They are also high-value targets for phishing and vendor impersonation because one successful compromise can open the environment. | GAP-013 because offboarding and access review are manual; GAP-017 because privileged access still depends too much on passwords; GAP-021 because informal change management increases the odds that a social-engineered change request will be accepted |
| Executives | Sensitive financial, legal, HR, strategic, and Board-facing information; approval authority for payments and policy decisions | Executives are prime BEC targets because they can authorize exceptions, wire transfers, and urgent requests. Their communication patterns are public and predictable. | GAP-017 because MFA is not deployed at scale; GAP-014 because sensitive data exports and unusual sharing are not well controlled; GAP-010 from the shadow-IT analysis because executive and marketing material can leak through unmanaged cloud paths |
| External contractors | Limited but often powerful access to vendor systems, support channels, and sometimes server or application administration | Contractors operate beyond MedDefense’s direct employment controls, which makes identity lifecycle and oversight harder. They are attractive because they often hold elevated access while remaining outside day-to-day supervision. | GAP-013 because access may remain after work ends; GAP-020 because third-party dependencies can sit outside MedDefense visibility; GAP-021 because change control is informal when vendors are involved |

### Surface Assessment Summary

The greatest risk today is the internal surface, because MedDefense’s flat network turns every internal foothold into broad lateral movement potential. A single compromised VPN session, phishing victim, or unattended workstation can reach critical services such as ehr-db-01, billing-srv-01, and the FortiGate management plane with too little resistance, and the lack of centralized monitoring means that movement may remain invisible long enough to matter. The external surface is still important because it creates the first foothold, but the internal surface is where that foothold becomes a hospital-wide incident. The human surface is the easiest way in, especially for phishing and BEC, but it becomes most dangerous when combined with the weak internal architecture and the control gaps documented in 1x00.

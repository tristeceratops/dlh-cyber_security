# The MedDefense Threat Actor Matrix

## Introduction

### Goal
Consolidate all threat actor analysis into a single prioritized reference matrix.

### Context
You have profiled the adversaries individually: ransomware operators in depth, insiders from five angles, social engineers across seven vectors, supply chain risk across five vendors. Now bring it together into one authoritative reference that answers the Board's original question: Who threatens MedDefense, and how much should we worry about each one ?

## Answer

### Assessment Basis

MedDefense is not a generic healthcare target. The organization combines a high-value clinical data set, a flat and weakly segmented network, a public-facing perimeter, incomplete MFA, manual offboarding, limited detection, and backups that are not isolated from production. The Data Map shows that protected health information, credentials, billing data, imaging, and executive or HR records all sit on systems that are reachable through a small number of high-value entry points. The Criticality Matrix and top assets therefore matter: ehr-srv-01, ehr-db-01, ad-dc-01, the MRI control workstation, and FortiGate-100F are the systems most likely to determine whether a security event becomes a hospital-wide incident.

The likelihood ratings below are relative to MedDefense specifically. Healthcare is persistently targeted by ransomware and credential theft because downtime affects patient care and regulated data can be monetized. At the same time, MedDefense has enough exposed infrastructure and weak detective coverage that even low-skill attackers can sometimes create serious operational impact.

### Top 3 Priority Ranking

#### 1. Ransomware Groups (Organized Crime)

This is the highest-priority threat because it combines high likelihood with catastrophic impact. Healthcare is one of the most consistently targeted sectors for ransomware because outages directly affect patient care and create strong pressure to pay. MedDefense is unusually exposed to the standard ransomware playbook: a likely edge or VPN entry point, no MFA at scale, a flat internal network, weak detection, and backups that are not truly isolated from production. A typical intrusion could begin with the FortiGate or another exposed service, move laterally to Active Directory and the EHR environment, and then encrypt ehr-srv-01 and ehr-db-01 while exfiltrating PHI for double extortion. That sequence would hit the organization where it is most vulnerable: clinical operations, regulated data, and recovery capability.

#### 2. Insider (Negligent)

This ranks second because it is both highly likely and already enabled by MedDefense’s operating model. The workforce has broad access to sensitive systems, training is imperfect, offboarding is manual, and detective controls are weak. In a hospital, negligence is not a rare edge case; it is a routine operational risk that becomes a security incident when it intersects with missing controls. An unlocked workstation at the nurse station, a misdirected file, a shared PACS login, or an unmanaged personal device can expose PHI or destabilize patient care without any hostile intent. The impact may not always be as dramatic as ransomware, but the frequency and the speed with which mistakes can spread make negligent insiders a top-tier threat.

#### 3. Unskilled / Opportunistic Attacker

This threat ranks third because MedDefense is sufficiently exposed that low-skill, high-volume attackers are likely to find something worth exploiting. The organization’s public perimeter, incomplete MFA, flat network, and limited monitoring mean that automated scanning, credential stuffing, or commodity exploit chains can still succeed. Even if the attacker is not sophisticated, the downstream impact can be serious: a compromised FortiGate or patient portal can become the first step toward Active Directory, the EHR, or other high-value systems. Opportunistic attackers do not need to be brilliant to create damage in a weak environment; they only need one working path and enough time before detection.

#### Close Fourth: Insider (Malicious)

A malicious insider remains a close fourth and should not be minimized. If the board’s concern is deliberate sabotage or targeted exfiltration, this actor can be more damaging than a low-skill outsider because the insider already has legitimate access, local knowledge, and a trusted position. However, the probability of a truly malicious insider is lower than the probability of ransomware or routine negligence, so it falls just outside the top three in an overall risk ranking.

### Bottom Line

MedDefense’s greatest external threat is organized ransomware. Its greatest internal threat is not a lone mastermind, but routine human error amplified by weak monitoring and weak identity governance. The organization should therefore treat the matrix as a prioritization tool: reduce the ransomware path first, but do not assume the biggest incidents will always start with an outside attacker.

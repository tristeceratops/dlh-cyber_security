# The Technical Vectors

## Introduction

### Goal
Identify and analyze the technical (non-human) attack vectors specific to MedDefense using concrete evidence from the network scan and posture assessment.

### Context
Social engineering gets you in the door. Technical vectors keep you in the building. The network scan from Project 1x00 revealed a landscape of exposed services, outdated software and absent controls that constitute a catalog of technical entry points and lateral movement opportunities.

The Sec+ framework (2.2) defines technical vectors as: vulnerable software, unsupported systems, unsecure networks (wireless, wired, Bluetooth), open service ports, default credentials and removable devices. Each of these exists in the MedDefense environment.

## Answer

### Technical Vector Assessment

The technical vector profile at MedDefense is defined less by one dramatic weakness than by the accumulation of exposed services, legacy systems, and flat-network trust. The organization has point controls in place, but the network scan and 1x00 posture artifacts show that attackers can still reach critical systems through outdated software, unsupported platforms, open management ports, weak credential practices, and unmanaged endpoints.

### Vector Prioritization

The most dangerous technical vectors are the ones that combine easy reach with broad blast radius: unsecure networks, open service ports, and unsupported systems. In MedDefense, the flat network is the multiplier that makes every other vector worse. A single exploit against billing-srv-01, ehr-db-01, or an exposed RDP/management interface does not stay local; it can become a launch point into identity infrastructure, EHR systems, medical devices, and backups.

The second tier of risk comes from default credentials and unmanaged endpoints. These are not always flashy, but they are highly practical for both insiders and commodity attackers because they exploit workflow convenience and governance gaps rather than exotic code execution. Finally, vulnerable software remains a persistent problem on exposed or legacy hosts, especially where patching is difficult or overdue.

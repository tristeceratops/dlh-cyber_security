# 9. The Data Map

## Introduction

### Goal
Identify, classify and trace the flow of sensitive data across its lifecycle states to identify protection gaps.

### Context
James Chen comes back from a meeting with the hospital's legal counsel. "Legal just reminded me that we handle protected health information for over 50,000 patients, plus financial data, plus employee records. They want to know: where exactly does our sensitive data live, how does it move, and is it protected at every step ?"

Data does not stay in one place. A patient's medical record is at rest in the EHR database, in transit when a physician accesses it from the nurse station over the network and in use when it is displayed on the screen. Each state requires different protections, and a gap in any state is a gap in the entire chain.

## Answer

| Data Category | Classification | At Rest (where) | In Transit (how) | In Use (by whom, on what) | Current Protection | Protection Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Patient medical records and EHR content | Restricted | EHR database and application stack on [ehr-db-01](./7-asset_registry.md) and [ehr-srv-01](./7-asset_registry.md); copies also appear in portal workflows and exports. | Accessed by clinicians over the internal network from nurse stations, workstations, and the patient portal; data moves across a flat hospital network and site links. | Physicians, nurses, and clinical staff on workstation fleets and nurse-station systems while viewing charts, orders, allergies, labs, and medications. | SSH key-based admin access on [ehr-srv-01](./4-control_inventory.md); endpoint protection on managed Windows workstations; password policy and account lockout; nightly backups for critical VMs. | Task 3 shows an EHR session left open at a nurse station, creating an immediate in-use exposure. The network is still effectively flat, no SIEM exists, MFA is limited, and [ehr-db-01](./7-asset_registry.md) exposes a broad 5432 service path, so PHI can be reached and altered without strong segmentation or continuous monitoring. |
| Medical imaging and radiology data | Restricted | PACS storage on [pacs-srv-01](./7-asset_registry.md) and MRI-related imaging workflows tied to [A-021](./7-asset_registry.md) and [A-022](./7-asset_registry.md). | MRI studies move from the control workstation to PACS over the internal network; the legacy MRI path depends on connectivity that is not well isolated. | Radiology staff on the MRI control workstation and imaging systems while acquiring, reviewing, and transferring studies. | Basic perimeter firewall controls exist; the MRI workstation cannot be reimaged because of certification constraints; compensating network controls were proposed for the legacy MRI environment. | Task 6 shows the MRI control workstation sits on the same VLAN as general workstations, so imaging data in transit is not meaningfully segmented from the rest of the hospital. The Windows XP Embedded workstation is unsupported, which makes image transfer and control traffic a high-risk path with weak technical containment. |
| Billing and claims data | Restricted | Billing server and associated records on [billing-srv-01](./7-asset_registry.md); related copies may exist in finance workflows and backups. | Claims, payer data, and billing exports move between finance staff, internal servers, and external insurer or clearinghouse workflows. | Finance and billing staff on HQ workstations and collaboration tools while processing claims, reimbursements, and revenue-cycle tasks. | Password policy, account lockout, endpoint protection on managed workstations, and nightly backups for critical systems. | [billing-srv-01](./7-asset_registry.md) is already flagged as EOL and has repeat compromise history. The environment lacks offsite or immutable recovery, so billing data is exposed to ransomware, tampering, and prolonged downtime if the local backup set is also affected. |
| Employee HR and payroll records | Confidential | Microsoft 365 tenant, HQ workstations, and any local HR files used by HR, legal, and finance. | HR files and payroll information move through email, cloud collaboration, and document sharing. | HR, legal, finance, and executives on HQ workstations, laptops, and Microsoft 365 apps. | Password policy, account lockout, endpoint protection on managed Windows systems, and user awareness training. | MFA is deployed only for James Chen's personal account, so most HR data paths still rely on password-only access. The lack of centralized alerting means phishing, account abuse, or inappropriate file sharing may persist without timely detection. |
| System credentials and authentication data | Restricted | Active Directory databases on [ad-dc-01](./7-asset_registry.md) and [ad-dc-02](./7-asset_registry.md), SSH keys on servers, and administrative credential records. | Credentials travel during AD logons, VPN sessions, SSH authentication, and device administration. | IT staff, clinicians, and admins on workstations, server consoles, switches, and remote-access sessions. | SSH key-based authentication on [ehr-srv-01](./4-control_inventory.md); root login disabled on Linux servers; password policy and account lockout; AD-backed badge and user authentication. | Task 3 shows network switch administrator credentials displayed openly in the network closet, which is a direct compromise of credential confidentiality in physical form. Shared accounts outside Radiology are unresolved, MFA is minimal, and VPN users can reportedly reach all services, making credentials the easiest route to broad compromise. |
| Audit logs and security records | Confidential | Local logs on firewalls, Windows servers, Linux servers, and EHR systems. | Log data is copied or reviewed manually rather than sent to a central monitoring platform. | IT staff and investigators reviewing logs on admin workstations or directly on the source devices. | Firewalls, servers, and the EHR generate logs; some firewall logging exists as a detective control. | [5-control_gaps.md](./5-control_gaps.md) shows there is no SIEM, no automated alerting, and no centralized security monitoring. Logs are kept locally and reviewed only when something breaks, so suspicious access to sensitive records can persist unnoticed. |
| Backup and recovery copies | Restricted | [backup-srv-01](./7-asset_registry.md) and [NAS-01](./7-asset_registry.md), with backups stored in the same server room as production systems. | Nightly backup jobs move data from production servers to the backup repository and NAS. | IT administrators during restore operations and recovery testing. | Nightly full backups with 14-day retention. | [5-control_gaps.md](./5-control_gaps.md) identifies no offsite or immutable backup strategy and no comprehensive disaster recovery capability. Because backups live in the same room as production, a fire, ransomware event, or physical breach could take out both the source data and the recovery copy. |
| Internal administrative and strategic documents | Confidential | Microsoft 365, HQ workstations, shared drives, and email boxes used by leadership, legal, finance, and operations. | Documents move through email, cloud sharing, and collaboration tools. | Executives and department leads on HQ workstations and laptops, primarily through Microsoft 365. | Password policy, account lockout, endpoint protection, and security awareness training. | These documents are widely distributed across cloud and endpoint systems, but the environment still lacks broad MFA, centralized monitoring, and role-specific detective controls. That makes strategic plans, contracts, and meeting material easy to expose through phishing or account compromise. |

Data in use is the weakest state overall. The clearest example is the unlocked EHR session at the nurse station from Task 3: patient data is visible on-screen, the workstation is already authenticated, and anyone nearby can view or alter restricted information without defeating a technical control. That gap is more serious than the storage-layer weaknesses because it directly combines confidentiality and integrity failure with immediate patient-safety impact.

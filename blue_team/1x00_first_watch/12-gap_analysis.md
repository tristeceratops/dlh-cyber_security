# The Gap Analysis

## Introduction

### Goal
Perform a formal gap analysis by systematically cross-referencing asset criticality, control coverage and identified weaknesses to produce a prioritized list of security gaps.

### Context
You have built three foundational documents: an Asset Registry with criticality ratings, a Data Map with protection gaps and a Complete Control Matrix with coverage assessments. Now you must bring them together.

"This is the analysis that matters," says James Chen. "Everything you've done so far is building blocks. This is where it becomes a story the Board can act on."

A gap analysis is not a list of problems. It is a structured assessment that connects what you need to protect (critical assets and data) with what protections exist (controls) and identifies where the distance between the two is unacceptable.

## Answer

### Gap Distribution Summary

- Critical gaps: 12
- High gaps: 4
- Medium gaps: 1
- Low gaps: 0

The asset categories with the most gaps are EHR/clinical systems, network/core infrastructure, backup and recovery, and shadow IT. The concentration is not random: MedDefense has more gaps where the asset is most critical and the data is most sensitive.

The control weakness is also concentrated in function rather than just category. The most common failures are missing or weak detective controls, followed by weak corrective/recovery capability, missing identity lifecycle controls, and weak patch governance for exposed systems. Preventive controls exist in several places, but they do not close the gap when data is in use, when assets are legacy or unmanaged, or when recovery depends on a single local backup set.

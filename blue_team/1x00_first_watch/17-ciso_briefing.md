MedDefense is not starting from zero, but our current security is not strong enough for a hospital that depends on electronic records, connected medical devices, and remote access. We have useful controls in place, but they are uneven, and several of our most important systems can still be reached too easily.

The most dangerous gap is that an attacker can get in with a stolen password, move across a flat network, and stay hidden because we do not have two-step login, network separation, or monitoring. In the real world, that is how a local intrusion becomes a hospital-wide ransomware event: records stop, appointments are cancelled, and patient care is disrupted for days or weeks.

1. **Require two-step login and automatic account shutdown.** Add two-step login for remote access, clinical systems, and administrators, and automatically disable accounts when staff leave. Estimated cost: $15,000-$40,000; timeline: within 1 month.

2. **Separate the network and patch exposed systems first.** Put critical systems on separate network segments, tighten access rules, and prioritize patches for the firewall, patient portal, and other internet-facing systems. Estimated cost: $20,000-$50,000; timeline: 1-3 months.

3. **Improve recovery and detection.** Use an outsourced monitoring service instead of buying an $80,000 log system this year, and move backups off the production network so ransomware cannot destroy both copies. Estimated cost: $25,000-$50,000; timeline: 1-3 months.

The $120,000 budget is an investment because it is far less than the cost of one major breach. One comparable hospital reported $3.2 million in recovery costs and $1.8 million in lost revenue from a ransomware event, and that does not count patient-safety risk or reputational damage.

If the Board takes no action, MedDefense should expect a more expensive breach, a longer outage, and a higher chance that patient care will be disrupted when the next attacker gets in.
